01 / Overview

The Project

KleanFleet came to me as a greenfield project ‚Äî a future car wash business owner needed a platform built from zero to manage everything: clients, vehicles, balances, invoices, and access restrictions across multiple locations.

What made this project exciting for me was that it wasn't just a UI job. I was involved from the very beginning, sitting in on discovery sessions, understanding the business model, mapping the operational flows, and then translating all of that complexity into a clean, role-based interface that actually makes sense to the people using it every day.

The platform needed to serve three completely different user types ‚Äî individual clients, fleet managers (PJ), and system administrators ‚Äî each with different needs, permissions, and mental models. My challenge was to make one coherent system that felt simple to each of them.

02 / Problem Statement

What Was Broken

Before this platform existed, managing a multi-location self-service car wash was a fragmented, manual process. Operators juggled spreadsheets, physical cards, and phone calls ‚Äî with no real visibility into what was happening, when, or why.

P1

Operational fragmentation. Managing multiple locations, vehicle fleets, and user roles was handled manually, with no central tool.

P2

Zero financial visibility. No balance tracking, no automated invoicing ‚Äî admins had to reconcile payments by hand.

P3

No usage controls. Fleet owners had no way to limit services per vehicle or per visit, leading to misuse and overspending.

P4

Role confusion. Individual users (PF) and corporate clients (PJ) were mixed together, causing permission errors and interface overload.

P5

All manual admin. Blocking vehicles, issuing invoices, managing locations ‚Äî all done by hand, all error-prone.

The Goal

Design a platform that gives every type of user ‚Äî individual, fleet manager, admin ‚Äî exactly the right level of control, clarity, and automation. No more, no less.

‚Äî How I Defined This

I sat in the discovery sessions with the future business owner. The goal wasn't just to understand what features they wanted ‚Äî it was to understand the business logic underneath. Why does a fleet manager need per-visit limits? What happens if a vehicle gets blacklisted mid-month? How does invoicing connect to accounting workflows?

That business context is what allowed me to design with real intent, not just make screens.

03 / Research

Understanding the People

I conducted in-depth interviews with the future platform owner ‚Äî someone who was both the client and the primary admin user. The goal was to go deep on their operational vision, not just gather feature requests.

How do you envision managing multiple washing boxes across different locations from one place?
What's your preferred method for identifying and charging clients ‚Äî by card, license plate, or both?
What kind of limits would you want to apply to users ‚Äî per visit, per vehicle, per month?
How should the invoicing and reporting process work to match your accounting flow?
What concerns do you have about misuse ‚Äî vehicles not in fleet, services used outside policy?
What would make you trust and actively use this platform on a daily basis?

Control is everything

The owner's biggest concern was misuse ‚Äî people using services they shouldn't. The system needed real-time visibility and the ability to block instantly.

üìä

06 / Design Decisions

Why I Made These Choices

Every design decision in this project had a reason rooted in the research. Here are the ones that shaped the final product the most.

01

Role-based dashboards instead of a single unified interface

Early in the process, I considered a unified layout with permission layers. But after mapping the journeys for each role, it was clear that Mihai (fleet manager) and Cristina (admin) have fundamentally different goals every time they log in. A shared interface would create cognitive overload for both. Separate dashboards with tailored navigation meant each user sees exactly what they need ‚Äî nothing more.

02

License plate AND card access ‚Äî not either/or

During the stakeholder interview, the owner described scenarios where a driver might not have a card with them, or a card might be shared across multiple vehicles. Supporting both methods ‚Äî license plate recognition and card-based access ‚Äî removed a major friction point for both clients and operators, without adding complexity to the admin layer.

03

Blacklist as a first-class feature, not an afterthought

In most existing tools, blocking a user is buried in settings. For Cristina, this was a daily concern ‚Äî she needed to blacklist a vehicle instantly, see the reason, who blocked it, and when. I gave the blacklist its own dedicated section in the admin navigation with full audit history, because that reflects how important it is to the business.

04

Automated invoicing with no manual steps

The accounting workflow was a key concern from day one. Rather than designing invoice creation as a manual action, I worked with the stakeholder to define automatic generation triggers ‚Äî end of month, per service, per client type. The admin only needs to review and download, never create.

05

Usage bars over raw numbers for fleet visibility

Fleet managers need to understand usage at a glance ‚Äî how much of the monthly limit has been used, per vehicle. Raw numbers require mental math. Visual usage bars with color thresholds (healthy ‚Üí warning ‚Üí exceeded) communicate the same information in a fraction of a second. This came directly from usability testing feedback.

10 / Reflections

What I Learned

This project pushed me in ways that pure UI work doesn't. Being present during discovery meant I had to understand accounting logic, K√§rcher's equipment constraints, and corporate fleet policies ‚Äî none of which I knew coming in. That context made every design decision sharper.

Key Takeaway 01

Business logic IS design logic

The more I understood how invoicing, blacklisting, and access control worked at a business level, the better the design got. Features that seemed like edge cases in a feature list turned out to be daily workflows for the owner. You can't design that from a brief alone.

Key Takeaway 02

Role separation saves everyone

The temptation was to build one flexible interface. The research showed clearly that this would have been the wrong call. Three distinct roles, three distinct needs ‚Äî respecting that boundary was the single biggest design decision in the project.

Key Takeaway 03

Test with the real user, not a surrogate

The usability testing sessions with the actual stakeholder revealed assumptions I didn't know I had made. The usage bar visualization, the blacklist placement, the invoice filter layout ‚Äî all of these changed based on real feedback, not guesswork.

What I'd Do Differently

Involve accounting earlier

The invoicing module went through the most iterations because I hadn't mapped the accounting workflows in enough detail during discovery. Next time, I'd dedicate a specific session to the financial flows ‚Äî not just the feature requirements ‚Äî before touching wireframes.
