01 / Overview

Why VetPet needed to exist

Pet owners face a fragmented, stressful experience managing their animal's health. Vaccination schedules, medical history, appointments ‚Äî scattered across paper, WhatsApp messages, and multiple apps.

VetPet is a single, unified mobile companion centralising every aspect of a pet's healthcare journey. I led the full design process ‚Äî from research through personas, IA, high-fidelity UI, and usability testing.

The core challenge: designing for two user types simultaneously ‚Äî the emotionally-invested pet owner who wants simplicity and reassurance, and the vet professional who needs structured, reliable data.

02 / Problem

What was broken?

"Pet owners lose track of their pet's health history. Vets can't access reliable records at appointments. The result? Repeated tests, missed vaccinations, and a lot of preventable stress."

‚Äî Synthesised from 8 user interviews
01
Fragmented health records
Medical history scattered across paper, photos, WhatsApp, and multiple apps ‚Äî no single reliable source of truth.
02
Appointment friction
Booking requires phone calls during business hours, leading to missed appointments and zero automated reminders.
03
Vaccination confusion
Owners miss deadlines because reminders don't exist or are buried in paper records they rarely check.
04
No vet communication
Between appointments, no reliable channel to ask questions or share symptom photos with a trusted professional.
05
Multi-pet chaos
Households with multiple pets juggle entirely separate records with no consolidated overview.
06
Emotional anxiety loop
Managing pet health provokes anxiety ‚Äî no app was designed with this emotional state at its core.
03 / Research

Listening first

8 interviews with pet owners + 4 sessions with veterinarians. Goal: uncover real workflows, not assumed ones.

Interview Questions
  • How do you currently track your pet's vaccinations and medical history?
  • Tell me about the last time you needed a specific vet record quickly. What happened?
  • How do you book vet appointments? What frustrates you most?
  • If your pet showed a worrying symptom between visits, what would you do?
  • How do you manage reminders for medications or check-ups?
  • What does "peace of mind" about your pet's health look like for you?

05 / Design Decisions

Why I chose what I chose

1
Persistent bottom nav ‚Äî not a hamburger
Mobile users in healthcare contexts are often anxious and one-handed. Persistent bottom navigation gives immediate access to core actions without cognitive overhead or hidden menus.
Mobile UX Principle
2
Orange accent ‚Äî warm, energetic, not clinical
Orange conveys warmth, energy and approachability ‚Äî what a pet owner needs when worried about their animal. Unlike clinical blue, it feels companion-like. The warm off-white (#FFF8F3) background reinforces this calming, non-medical atmosphere.
Colour Psychology ¬∑ Brand
3
Pet profile as health dashboard, not a contact card
Users felt most reassured when they could see their pet's entire status at a glance ‚Äî vaccinations current, next appointment visible, zero active alerts. The profile screen became the most loved screen in all testing sessions.
IA ¬∑ Emotional Design
4
3-tier reminder system with per-pet controls
To combat notification fatigue: informational (orange), advisory (amber), urgent (red). Users can customise urgency per pet. This design decision was estimated to reduce notification dismissal rates by 60% vs a flat system.
Notification Design ¬∑ User Control
5
Shareable PDF export ‚Äî the bridge between users
Owners needed warmth; vets needed structured data. Rather than forcing one UI to serve both, a clean one-tap PDF export satisfied vets completely and gave owners a way to feel prepared for every appointment.
Cross-User Solution ¬∑ IA

08 / Reflections

What I learned

üí°Emotional context shapes everything
Pet owners open this app during anxious moments. This shaped every decision ‚Äî colour, hierarchy, loading states, empty states. Designing for emotion requires genuine empathy, not just research.
üß™Test earlier, test more
My initial IA placed health records under a "documents" metaphor ‚Äî which tested poorly. Users expected a timeline. Earlier wireframe testing would have saved a full redesign cycle.
‚öñÔ∏èBalancing two user types
Owners wanted warmth; vets wanted structure. The PDF export was the breakthrough ‚Äî it satisfied both without forcing a UI compromise or diluting either experience.
üîÑWhat I'd do differently
I'd invest more in the vet-facing experience. A fully realised product needs a separate vet portal with its own design language ‚Äî a natural phase 2 I'd love to explore.
